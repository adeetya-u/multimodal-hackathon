## Surgery to the Knee


## Total Knee Arthroplasty

Over 700,000 TKAs are performed each year in the United States.56 Because TKA involves the cutting and cementing of two long bones, the femur, and tibia, these procedures have a painful recovery with a high incidence of chronic pain following both primary and revision procedures. It is important to create an appropriate postoperative analgesia plan to ensure mobility and facilitate range-of-motion therapy.


## Positioning and Blood Loss

Knee arthroplasties and arthroscopies are performed supine to allow easy access to and evaluation of the knee joint in extension and flexion. A thigh tourniquet will minimize bleeding and improve surgical visualization; however, the tourniquet may not optimally compress the arteries in patients with severe atherosclerosis. Tourniquets should be used with caution in patients with active infections or thromboses in the lower extremity as exsanguination may cause systemic spread. The antifibrinolytic TXA can be safely utilized to decrease blood loss and blood transfusion.70 As described earlier for hip surgery, controlled hypotension is a controversial option if a tourniquet cannot be applied.


## Analgesia for Total Knee Arthroplasty

The optimal analgesia for TKA is a complex and evolving topic (Table 51-4). Regional anesthesia improves patient pain outcomes compared to traditional opioid regimens. Epidural catheters with a continuous infusion of dilute local anesthetic and low-dose opioids can provide excellent pain

<!-- page:4385 -->

control, particularly when paired with patient-controlled epidural analgesia. Management of an epidural catheter for postoperative analgesia must account for pharmacologic venous thromboprophylaxis. Ultrasound-guided regional anesthesia has led to a significant increase in the use of peripheral nerve blocks and catheters as components of postoperative analgesic regimens. Intrathecal morphine can be utilized in addition to peripheral nerve blocks for postoperative analgesia; however, prolonged postoperative monitoring is required due to the risk of delayed respiratory depression. A balanced multimodal analgesic regimen can include pharmacologic treatment with anti-inflammatories, acetaminophen, opioids, and medications that manage neuropathic components of pain, such as pregabalin. Such a multimodal approach can maximize analgesic efficacy while minimizing side effects.

The femoral, sciatic, and obturator nerves provide sensation to the knee joint. The most commonly utilized peripheral nerve blocks are the LPB, fascia iliaca (3-in-1 block), FNB, and adductor canal (or saphenous) nerve blocks (see Chapter 36). These blocks can be combined with a sciatic nerve block, an obturator nerve block, or an infiltration between the popliteal artery and the capsule of the posterior knee (IPACK) block. Literature and clinical practice continue to evolve regarding which blocks or combination of blocks best facilitate rehabilitation and postoperative mobilization, thereby reducing time to hospital discharge, enhancing cost-effectiveness, and reducing the risk for complications such as ambulation-related falls.

Historically, FNB was currently considered the peripheral nerve block of choice for TKA analgesia because it has a low complication profile and is easy to perform with nerve stimulation or ultrasound guidance. However, although its correlation is disputed, one drawback to FNB is quadriceps weakness (up to 80%) and associated fall risk.

Recently, motor-sparing blocks such as the adductor canal block (ACB) have demonstrated a similar analgesic profile to FNB, but with the added benefit of increased mobility by sparing quadriceps strength. Performing the block requires ultrasound guidance and the use of a low local anesthetic volume within the adductor canal can minimize motor blockade of the vastus medialis.

The sciatic nerve provides sensation to the posterior compartment of the knee and, in combination with an FNB, can reduce opioid consumption after TKA.71 Sciatic nerve blockade causes a "foot drop" that can increase

<!-- page:4387 -->

postoperative fall risk and delay diagnosis of common peroneal nerve palsy, a complication seen with large valgus corrections of the knee. A selective tibial nerve block is an alternative that provides similar analgesia without the corresponding foot drop.72

As noted above, the IPACK block is a motor-sparing alternative to the sciatic nerve block for posterior knee pain73,74 that targets the 12 articular branches of both the anterior and posterior knee capsule. These blocks have demonstrated analgesic efficacy when performed in addition to ACB or periarticular injections. Transient foot drops are rare but can occur if local anesthesia is deposited near the sciatic nerve.

Periarticular injections (local infiltration technique) with cocktails of medications (see Surgery to the Hip and Pelvis, Anesthesia Technique) performed by the surgeon can reduce LOS and improve resting pain scores compared to placebo; however, they do not seem to provide greater benefit than regional anesthesia techniques. Liposomal bupivacaine can be added to periarticular injections to prolong the effect of the local anesthetic, although the benefit of liposomal bupivacaine over standard local anesthetics is not conclusive and both the safety profile and cost should be considered.


## Ambulatory Knee Surgery

Ambulatory knee surgery has increased because health care costs have encouraged outpatient management of less complex cases. An optimal anesthetic in this setting has a rapid onset and fast offset with minimal side effects to prevent prolonged postanesthesia care unit stays or unexpected overnight admissions. NA results in a lower rate of nausea and vomiting than general anesthesia and can improve readiness to discharge. However, in a practice with rapid turnover time or limited postanesthesia care unit capacity, the need to wait for block resolution may not be practical. In such settings, general anesthesia with multimodal antinausea prophylaxis may be appropriate.

In performing a spinal anesthetic in an ambulatory setting, a short-to-intermediate-acting local anesthetic should be utilized. Intrathecal lidocaine causes a high rate of transient neurologic symptoms (TNS) and is rarely used. Historically, the reported incidence of TNS with 1.5% mepivacaine was also significant; however, with the ubiquitous use of anti-inflammatories such as

<!-- page:4388 -->

ketorolac and dexamethasone for antiemetic prophylaxis, the incidence is quite low.75 TNS is rare with 2% chloroprocaine and 0.5% bupivacaine, although bupivacaine may not be appropriate for ambulatory cases owing to its longer duration. Short-acting narcotics (e.g., fentanyl) can be added to spinal blocks to increase anesthetic potency, but the resulting pruritus may not be tolerated. In a large multicenter retrospective study of ambulatory TKA, there was no difference in complications associated with anesthetic type, however, NA was associated with higher same-day discharge, lower pain, lower opioid use, and a lower incidence of postoperative nausea and vomiting.50

Anterior cruciate ligament (ACL) reconstruction is a common ambulatory procedure performed with a cadaveric allograft or an autograft from the patellar tendon or hamstring. The autograft is preferred in younger and more active patients, but the patellar tendon and hamstring grafts cause significant postoperative pain. An epidural or spinal block can provide intraoperative anesthesia in combination with an FNB, ACB, or catheters for analgesia. However, FNB is not superior to multimodal analgesia and may prolong discharge. In addition, quadriceps weakness can be a complication of ACL reconstructions owing to muscle atrophy and activation failure, and FNB may cloud the clinical picture. An ACB can provide similar analgesia to an FNB after ACL surgery without causing quadriceps weakness.76 The IPACK block may provide analgesic benefit for ACL surgery as well, although more randomized controlled trials are needed to determine the utility.
