## CAMPBELL'S CONCEPTS

Precautions and Recommendations in
Anteromedial Parapatellar Approach

When any anteromedial approach is made, including one for meniscectomy, find and protect the infrapatellar branch of the saphenous nerve. No single incision on the anteromedial aspect of the knee can avoid it for sure because several variations exist in the location of the infrapatellar branch (Fig. 1.44).

If contracture of the quadriceps prevents sufficient ex-
posure, detach or osteotomize the tibial tuberosity, and
reattach it later with a screw or, alternatively, with
three lag screws engaging the posterior tibial cortex as
described by Fernandez. This technique achieves rigid
fixation and allows early postoperative rehabilitation.

If the parapatellar approach is used for total knee arthroplasty (nonresurfaced patella), repair of the medial patellofemoral ligament is recommended at closure to prevent significant medial capsular dehiscence and resultant loading of the lateral patellar facet and increased anterior knee pain (Keshmiri et al.).

## SUBVASTUS (SOUTHERN)
ANTEROMEDIAL APPROACH TO
THE KNEE

Problems with patellar dislocation, subluxation, and osteo-
necrosis after total knee arthroplasty performed through
an anteromedial parapatellar approach led to the rediscov-
ery of the subvastus or Southern anteromedial approach
first described by Erkes in 1929. This approach preserves
the vascularity of the patella by sparing the intramuscular
articular branch of the descending genicular artery and pre-
serves the quadriceps tendon, supplying more stability to
the patellofemoral joint in total knee arthroplasty. NOTE!
This approach cannot be used in patients with a thigh
girth of approximately 55 cm, because the patella cannot
be everted during total knee arthroplasty. The approach is
useful for lesser anteromedial and medial knee procedures.
The relative contraindications are:

▪ Earlier major knee arthroplasty
▪ Patient weight > 200 lb
▪ Thigh girth > 55 cm in patients undergoing total knee arthroplasty


## TECHNIQUE 1.43


## (Erkes, as described by Hofmann, Plaster, and Murdock)

1. Exsanguinate the limb and inflate the tourniquet with the knee flexed to at least 90 degrees to prevent tenodesis of the extensor mechanism.
2. Make a straight anterior skin incision, beginning 8 cm above the patella, carrying it distally just medial and 2 cm distal to the tibial tubercle.

3. Incise the superficial fascia slightly medial to the patella (Fig. 1.45A) and bluntly dissect it off the vastus medialis muscle fascia down to the muscle insertion (Fig. 1.45B).
4. Find the inferior edge of the vastus medialis and bluntly dissect it off the periosteum and intermuscular septum for 10 cm proximal to the adductor tubercle.
5. Find the tendinous insertion of the muscle on the me-
dial patellar retinaculum (Fig. 1.45C), lift the vastus
medialis muscle anteriorly, and perform an L-shaped
arthrotomy beginning medially through the vastus in-
sertion on the medial patellar retinaculum and carrying
it along the medial edge of the patella.
6. Partially release the medial edge of the patellar tendon
and evert the patella laterally with the knee extended
(Fig. 1.45D).


## ANTEROLATERAL APPROACH TO THE KNEE

Usually, the anterolateral approach is not as satisfactory as the anteromedial one for arthroplasty, primarily because it is more difficult to displace the patella medially than later-
ally. It also requires a longer incision, and often the patellar tendon must be partially freed subperiosteally or subcorti-
cally. However, the approach allows extension into a lateral approach to the femur for intraarticular fractures where
joint access is needed.


## TECHNIQUE 1.44


## (KOCHER)

1. Begin the incision 7.5 cm proximal to the patella at the insertion of the vastus lateralis muscle into the quadri-
ceps tendon; continue it distally along the lateral border
of this tendon, the patella, and the patellar tendon; and
end it 2.5 cm distal to the tibial tuberosity.
2. Deepen the dissection through the joint capsule.
3. Retract the patella medially, with the tendons attached
to it, and expose the articular surface of the joint. Avoid
injury to the anterior horn of the lateral meniscus.


## ■ Approach for total knee arthroplasty in fixed valgus knees

Satish et al. found the modified Keblish approach useful in
total knee arthroplasty in patients with fixed valgus knees.
The approach relies on a quadriceps snip and coronal
Z-plasty of the lateral retinacular capsule complex. The lateral
retinacular complex is separated into two layers: deep (cap-
sule and synovium) and superficial. The lateral parapatellar
arthrotomy is performed 3 to 7 cm lateral to the patella, and
the deep and superficial layers are separated with dissection
carried medially toward the patella. The superficial layer is
kept attached to the patella, and the deep layer stays attached
to the iliotibial band. At closure, the layers are approximated
in an expanded fashion (Fig. 1.47).

## CAMPBELL'S CONCEPTS

Considering the Median Septum in Posterolateral and Posteromedial Approaches to the Knee

A median septum separating the posterior aspect of the knee into two compartments is present in some patients. The posterior cruciate ligament is extrasynovial and projects anteriorly in the septum, contributing to the partition between the two posterior compartments. The middle genicular artery courses anteriorly in the septum to nourish the tissues of the intercondylar notch of the femur (Fig. 1.50).

The median septum assumes significant importance when exploring the posterior aspect of the knee for a loose body or when posterior draining of the joint is necessary for pyogenic arthritis. In the latter, remember to drain both posterior compartments.


## MEDIAL APPROACHES TO THE KNEE AND SUPPORTING STRUCTURES

Usually, the entire medial meniscus can be excised through a
medial parapatellar incision about 5 cm long. If the posterior
horn of the meniscus cannot be excised through this incision,
a separate posteromedial Henderson approach can be made
(see Fig. 1.49). The anterior and posterior compartments
may be entered, however, through an approach in which only
one incision is made through the skin, but two incisions are
used through the deeper structures; this type of approach is
rarely necessary.

## LATERAL APPROACHES TO THE KNEE AND SUPPORTING STRUCTURES

Lateral approaches allow good exposure for complete exci-
sion of the lateral meniscus. They do not require division or
release of the fibular collateral ligament. Several lateral ap-
proaches to the knee are described.

## TECHNIQUE 1.50


## (BRUSER)

1. Place the patient supine and drape the limb to allow full flexion of the knee. Flex the knee fully so that the foot rests flat on the operating table.

2. Begin the incision anteriorly where the patellar tendon crosses the lateral joint line, continue it posteriorly along the joint line, and end it at an imaginary line extending from the proximal end of the fibula to the lateral femo-
ral condyle (Fig. 1.54A).

3. Incise the subcutaneous tissue and expose the iliotibial
band, the fibers of which are parallel with the skin incision
when the knee is fully flexed (Fig. 1.54B). Split the band
in line with its fibers. Posteriorly, take care to avoid injuring
the relaxed fibular collateral ligament; it is protected by
areolar tissue, which separates it from the iliotibial band.

4. Retract the margins of the iliotibial band; this is possible to achieve without much force because the band is relaxed when the knee and hip are flexed.

5. Locate the lateral inferior genicular artery, which lies outside the synovium between the collateral ligament and the posterolateral aspect of the meniscus.

6. Incise the synovium. The lateral meniscus lies in the depth
of the incision and can be excised completely (Fig. 1.54C).

7. With the knee flexed 90 degrees, close the synovium
(Fig. 1.54D); and with the knee extended, close the
deep fascia.

## EXTENSILE APPROACH TO THE KNEE

Fernandez described an extensile anterior approach to the
knee based on an anterolateral approach that allows easy
access to the medial and lateral compartments in the fol-
lowing ways:

1. An extensive osteotomy of the tibial tuberosity allows proximal reflection of the patella, patellar tendon, and retropatellar fat pad.

2. Transecting the anterior horn and anterior portion of the coronary ligament of the medial meniscus or the lateral meniscus or both as necessary achieves adequate exposure.

This approach may be used for tumor resection, liga-
ment reconstruction, fracture reduction and fixation, and
adult reconstructive procedures. Part or all this approach may
be used as necessary to achieve the required exposure. Rigid
screw fixation of the tibial tuberosity engaging the posterior
cortex of the tibia allows early postoperative knee motion.

Perry et al. first reported transection of the anterior
horn of the lateral meniscus to aid exposure of lateral tib-
ial plateau fractures. Alternatively, the articular surface of
either tibial plateau can be approached with submeniscal
exposure by releasing the peripheral attachment of the
meniscus at the coronary ligament and by elevating the
meniscus, as described by Gossling and Peterson.

## CAMPBELL'S CONCEPTS

Recommendations and Precautions in
Posterior Approaches to the Knee

The posterior midline approach involves structures that,
if damaged, can produce a permanent, serious disability.
A thorough knowledge of the anatomy of the popliteal
space is essential; see Figure 1.60, which shows the
relationship of the flexion crease to the joint line, and

around the knee posteriorly.
